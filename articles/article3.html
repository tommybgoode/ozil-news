Can eyes really be offside?

By Tom Blackbourn

May 11, 2020

His eyes are offside. His eyes are offside. Mesut Özil, his eyes are offside! We've all heard the chants but is there any truth to Chelsea fans' claims that Özil's eyes are causing him to be caught offside. Editor in Chief Tom Blackbourn gets straight to the facts.

The Offside Rule

The offide rule in association football requires two conditions to be met for a player to be deemed in an offside position:

  1. Any part of the player's head, body or feet is in the opponents' half of the field (excluding the half-way line).

  2. Any part of the player's head, body or feet is closer to the opponents' goal line than both the ball and the second-last opponent.

From this perspective at least it would seem there could be some truth to the claims. Eyes are certainly part of the head. But is this really what the Chelsea fans are driving at? Granted, being part of the head it can't be denied that the eyes can be offside along with the chin, the ears, the nose. But could Özil's eyes alone cause him to be offside?

Offside by a Nose

We have established that any part of the head can be offside. But can only the eyes be offside? Medically, the most sticky out part of a human head is the nose, with the average male nose protruding 2.6cm. The normal amount of ocular protrusion as measured (with an exophthalmometer) from the lateral orbital rim to the corneal apex is 14–21 mm in adults; protrusion greater than 21 mm is abnormal. Note that this is from the orbital rim. Even with a large protrusion of 26mm (equal to the average nose length) the eyes would still not extend beyond the nose.

Perhaps a more helpful comparison is the world record holder Kim Goodman (USA), who can pop her eyeballs to a protrusion of 12 mm (0.47 in) beyond her eye sockets. This makes it almost inconceivable that the eyes can be offside without the nose also being offside.


So there you have it. His eyes are offside. His eyes are offside. Mesut Özil, his eyes are offside! And also his nose.
